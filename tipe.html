Project Overview

This electronics-focused project aimed to reduce wind noise in ski helmets while preserving ambient sounds for safety. The key objective was to recreate a synchronous detection system‚Äîtypically used to extract weak signals buried in noise‚Äîusing basic electronic components like resistors, capacitors, and op-amps.

The final goal was to miniaturize the circuit for real-life integration inside a ski helmet.

Initial Experimentation

I began by simplifying the input signal using a tuning fork to simulate a pure frequency tone, while replicating wind noise using a fan. This setup allowed me to focus on signal isolation techniques in realistic conditions.

The wind signal was approximated as noise above 400 Hz, and the human voice range (100‚Äì400 Hz) was targeted for preservation.

Optimizing Signal Filtering

The initial low-pass filter (1st order) was insufficient. To improve accuracy, I added an amplification stage and a 2nd-order active low-pass filter (Rauch structure). This significantly improved the signal-to-noise ratio and filtering performance.

The final system showed a 23% improvement in signal clarity. I then continued the study by aiming to miniaturize the circuit.
